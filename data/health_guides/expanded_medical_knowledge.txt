MediSense AI Expanded Medical Knowledge Guide

Important safety principle:
This guide is educational. It helps with symptom understanding, prevention, self-care, and deciding when to seek medical care. It must not be used to confirm a diagnosis, prescribe medicine, or replace a qualified clinician.

Emergency red flags:
Seek urgent medical care now for chest pain or pressure, severe shortness of breath, blue lips, fainting, confusion, seizure, sudden weakness on one side of the body, face drooping, trouble speaking, severe allergic reaction with swelling of lips or throat, severe bleeding, black stools with weakness, vomiting blood, severe dehydration, stiff neck with fever, severe headache unlike usual, severe abdominal pain, poisoning, serious injury, pregnancy with bleeding or severe pain, or fever in a very young infant.

How to ask better follow-up questions:
Ask how long symptoms have been present, age, temperature if fever is present, severity of pain, location of pain, recent travel, sick contacts, pregnancy, chronic diseases, current medicines, allergies, hydration and urination, and whether symptoms are improving or worsening.

Fever:
Fever can happen with viral infections, flu, dengue, malaria, typhoid, pneumonia, urinary infection, heat illness, or other inflammation. Home care includes rest, fluids, light food, avoiding overheating, and monitoring temperature. See a doctor if fever lasts more than 3 days, is very high, returns repeatedly, or comes with rash, breathing trouble, stiff neck, confusion, severe weakness, persistent vomiting, severe headache, chest pain, or dehydration.

Common cold and flu:
Common cold often causes runny nose, sneezing, mild sore throat, cough, and low fever. Flu is usually more sudden and can cause high fever, body pain, chills, headache, and strong fatigue. Prevention includes hand washing, avoiding close contact when sick, masks in crowded places during outbreaks, good sleep, and vaccination where appropriate. Medical review is needed for breathing trouble, chest pain, persistent high fever, worsening cough, dehydration, elderly patients, pregnancy, infants, or people with asthma, heart disease, diabetes, or weak immunity.

COVID-like respiratory illness:
Symptoms can include fever, cough, sore throat, blocked nose, tiredness, headache, body ache, loss of smell or taste, diarrhea, or breathing difficulty. Prevention includes staying home when sick, hand hygiene, masks in high-risk settings, ventilation, and vaccination as advised locally. Seek care for low oxygen, worsening breathlessness, chest pain, confusion, bluish lips, or high-risk patients.

Cough:
Dry cough can occur with viral illness, allergies, asthma, acid reflux, pollution, or medication side effects. Wet cough may suggest mucus from infection or bronchitis. Warning signs include coughing blood, chest pain, breathlessness, wheezing, fever for several days, weight loss, night sweats, or cough lasting more than 3 weeks.

Sore throat:
Sore throat is commonly viral, but bacterial infection may be possible when fever, swollen neck glands, no cough, and white patches on tonsils are present. Home care includes warm fluids, salt-water gargles, voice rest, and hydration. Seek care for difficulty breathing or swallowing, drooling, severe one-sided pain, neck swelling, rash, or symptoms lasting more than a week.

Asthma and wheezing:
Asthma can cause wheezing, cough, chest tightness, and shortness of breath, often triggered by dust, smoke, exercise, cold air, infection, pets, or pollen. Prevention includes avoiding triggers, using prescribed inhalers correctly, keeping an asthma action plan, and getting medical review if rescue inhaler use increases. Emergency care is needed if the person cannot speak full sentences, lips look blue, breathing is very hard, or symptoms do not improve with prescribed rescue treatment.

Pneumonia:
Pneumonia can cause fever, cough, chest pain while breathing, fast breathing, breathlessness, chills, weakness, or confusion in older adults. It is more serious in children, elderly people, pregnant people, and those with diabetes, heart disease, lung disease, or weak immunity. Medical care is important because pneumonia may need examination, oxygen check, chest imaging, or antibiotics if bacterial.

Headache:
Common causes include tension, dehydration, lack of sleep, migraine, sinus congestion, eye strain, fever, or high blood pressure. Migraine may include one-sided throbbing pain, nausea, sensitivity to light or sound, and worsening with activity. Urgent care is needed for sudden worst headache, headache after head injury, fever with stiff neck, weakness, confusion, seizure, vision loss, pregnancy with severe headache, or new headache after age 50.

Migraine:
Migraine prevention includes regular sleep, hydration, meals on time, limiting triggers, reducing screen strain, stress management, and tracking attacks. Seek medical advice if migraines are frequent, changing pattern, very severe, associated with neurological symptoms, or interfering with daily life.

Dizziness:
Dizziness can happen from dehydration, low blood sugar, low blood pressure, anemia, vertigo, ear problems, anxiety, heat exposure, or heart rhythm problems. Seek urgent care for fainting, chest pain, weakness on one side, slurred speech, severe headache, persistent vomiting, severe dehydration, or dizziness after head injury.

Dehydration:
Signs include thirst, dry mouth, dark urine, low urine output, dizziness, tiredness, sunken eyes, and fast heartbeat. Oral fluids and oral rehydration solution can help mild dehydration. Urgent care is needed for confusion, fainting, very low urine, persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, blood in stool, babies with fewer wet diapers, or elderly patients with weakness.

Diarrhea and vomiting:
Common causes include food poisoning, viral gastroenteritis, contaminated water, medication effects, and digestive disorders. Home care includes oral rehydration, light food, avoiding alcohol and oily food, and hand hygiene. See a doctor for blood in stool, severe abdominal pain, persistent fever, signs of dehydration, diarrhea lasting more than 2-3 days, pregnancy, infants, elderly people, or weak immunity.

Food poisoning:
Food poisoning may cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, stomach cramps, fever, and weakness after unsafe food or water. Prevention includes washing hands, cooking food thoroughly, avoiding stale food, safe storage, clean water, and separating raw and cooked food. Medical care is needed for blood in stool, severe dehydration, high fever, neurological symptoms, pregnancy, infants, elderly people, or symptoms after mushrooms/chemicals.

Acidity and indigestion:
Acidity can cause burning in chest or upper abdomen, sour burps, bloating, nausea, or throat irritation. Helpful habits include smaller meals, avoiding late-night heavy meals, limiting spicy/oily foods, caffeine and alcohol, not lying down right after meals, and weight control. Chest pain, sweating, breathlessness, pain spreading to arm/jaw, vomiting blood, black stools, difficulty swallowing, or unexplained weight loss need medical review.

Abdominal pain:
Mild stomach pain may come from gas, indigestion, constipation, food poisoning, acidity, or viral infection. Urgent care is needed for severe or worsening pain, right lower abdominal pain, rigid abdomen, persistent vomiting, blood in vomit or stool, fever with severe pain, pregnancy, fainting, chest pain, or pain after injury.

Constipation:
Constipation may improve with water, fiber-rich foods, fruits, vegetables, regular walking, and regular toilet routine. Seek medical help for severe abdominal pain, vomiting, blood in stool, unexplained weight loss, new constipation in older adults, or no stool/gas with swelling.

Diabetes:
Common symptoms include frequent urination, increased thirst, increased hunger, fatigue, blurry vision, slow wound healing, repeated infections, and unexplained weight loss. Prevention and control include healthy diet, regular activity, weight management, sleep, not smoking, routine blood sugar checks, and taking prescribed medicines as directed by a doctor. Urgent care is needed for confusion, deep breathing, severe weakness, repeated vomiting, very high sugar readings, or signs of severe low sugar such as sweating, shaking, confusion, or fainting.

High blood pressure:
Hypertension often has no symptoms. Some people may notice headache, dizziness, chest discomfort, or nosebleeds, but normal feeling does not mean BP is normal. Prevention includes less salt, regular exercise, healthy weight, sleep, stress control, avoiding tobacco, limiting alcohol, and routine BP monitoring. Urgent care is needed for chest pain, severe headache, breathlessness, weakness, confusion, vision changes, or very high BP with symptoms.

Heart health:
Heart attack symptoms can include chest pressure, pain spreading to arm/jaw/back, sweating, nausea, breathlessness, fainting, or unusual fatigue. Women, elderly people, and people with diabetes can have less typical symptoms. Prevention includes no smoking, BP/sugar/cholesterol control, exercise, balanced diet, sleep, stress management, and regular checkups.

Anemia:
Anemia can cause tiredness, weakness, pale skin, dizziness, shortness of breath on exertion, fast heartbeat, headache, or cold hands and feet. Causes include low iron, B12 or folate deficiency, blood loss, heavy periods, pregnancy, chronic disease, or poor diet. Medical evaluation is important before taking supplements long term.

Thyroid problems:
Hypothyroidism can cause weight gain, tiredness, constipation, dry skin, hair fall, feeling cold, slow heart rate, and irregular periods. Hyperthyroidism can cause weight loss, fast heartbeat, sweating, anxiety, tremor, heat intolerance, loose stools, and sleep problems. Diagnosis needs blood tests, so users should consult a doctor for persistent symptoms.

Urinary tract infection:
UTI symptoms include burning urination, frequent urination, urgency, lower abdominal pain, cloudy or smelly urine, fever, or back pain. Drink fluids, do not hold urine for long, and maintain hygiene. Medical care is needed for fever, back pain, pregnancy, male patients, blood in urine, diabetes, kidney disease, recurrent UTI, or symptoms lasting more than 24-48 hours.

Kidney stone:
Kidney stones may cause severe flank pain that comes in waves, pain moving to groin, nausea, vomiting, blood in urine, or burning urine. Urgent care is needed for fever, inability to pass urine, uncontrolled pain, repeated vomiting, single kidney, pregnancy, or known kidney disease.

Skin rash and allergy:
Rashes can happen due to allergy, infection, heat, eczema, insect bites, medicines, or autoimmune conditions. Mild itching can improve by avoiding triggers, gentle skin care, and not scratching. Emergency care is needed for swelling of lips/tongue/throat, breathing trouble, widespread blistering, purple rash, fever with rash, rash after a new medicine, or rash with severe illness.

Eye symptoms:
Red eye can be due to allergy, infection, dryness, injury, or irritation. Urgent care is needed for eye pain, vision loss, light sensitivity, chemical exposure, injury, severe headache with eye pain, or contact lens users with red painful eye.

Ear pain:
Ear pain may be due to infection, wax, throat infection, dental pain, or pressure changes. Medical review is advised for fever, discharge, hearing loss, severe pain, dizziness, swelling behind ear, diabetes, or symptoms in small children.

Back pain:
Most back pain is muscular and improves with gentle movement, posture care, heat/ice, and avoiding heavy lifting. Urgent care is needed for weakness or numbness in legs, loss of bladder or bowel control, fever, history of cancer, major injury, unexplained weight loss, or pain with pregnancy complications.

Joint pain:
Joint pain can come from strain, injury, arthritis, infection, gout, viral illness, or autoimmune disease. Seek care for swollen hot joint, fever, inability to bear weight, injury, deformity, severe pain, rash, or persistent morning stiffness.

Dengue:
Dengue can cause high fever, severe headache, pain behind eyes, muscle and joint pain, rash, nausea, vomiting, and weakness. Warning signs include severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, bleeding from gums or nose, black stools, restlessness, drowsiness, cold clammy skin, or difficulty breathing. Prevention focuses on mosquito control, repellents, long sleeves, and removing standing water.

Malaria:
Malaria can cause fever with chills, sweating, headache, body pain, nausea, vomiting, fatigue, and sometimes jaundice or confusion. It is important to test and treat early. Prevention includes mosquito nets, repellents, full sleeves, and avoiding mosquito bites, especially during travel to malaria areas.

Typhoid:
Typhoid can cause prolonged fever, headache, weakness, stomach pain, constipation or diarrhea, poor appetite, and sometimes rash. Prevention includes safe water, hand washing, proper sanitation, avoiding unsafe street food or raw foods in high-risk areas, and vaccination where advised.

Heat exhaustion and heatstroke:
Heat exhaustion causes heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, cramps, and thirst. Move to shade, cool the body, and drink fluids if awake. Heatstroke is an emergency with very high body temperature, confusion, fainting, seizure, hot skin, or altered behavior.

Mental health stress and anxiety:
Stress and anxiety can cause racing thoughts, palpitations, sweating, sleep problems, stomach upset, headache, chest tightness, or breathlessness. Helpful steps include slow breathing, regular routine, sleep, exercise, limiting caffeine, talking to someone trusted, and professional counseling. Emergency help is needed for self-harm thoughts, feeling unsafe, severe panic with chest pain or fainting, or inability to function.

Sleep:
Poor sleep can worsen headache, acidity, blood pressure, sugar control, mood, immunity, and concentration. Good sleep habits include fixed sleep time, morning sunlight, less caffeine late in the day, screen breaks before bed, and a calm bedroom.

Healthy lifestyle:
A prevention plan should include balanced meals, enough protein, fruits and vegetables, water, regular activity, no tobacco, limited alcohol, vaccination, sleep, stress management, dental hygiene, and regular checkups for blood pressure, sugar, cholesterol, and weight.

Vaccination:
Vaccines help prevent several serious infections. Users should follow local medical advice for influenza, COVID, tetanus, hepatitis, HPV, pneumonia, travel vaccines, childhood vaccines, and pregnancy-related vaccines.

Child health:
Children need medical care sooner than adults if there is fast breathing, poor feeding, dehydration, persistent vomiting, lethargy, bluish lips, seizure, fever in infants, rash with fever, stiff neck, severe pain, or worsening symptoms. Do not give adult medicines to children without a clinician.

Pregnancy:
Pregnant users should seek medical advice for fever, abdominal pain, bleeding, severe vomiting, swelling with headache, vision changes, reduced baby movement, painful urination, breathlessness, chest pain, or high BP symptoms. Avoid self-medicating in pregnancy.

Medicine safety:
Do not prescribe specific medicine or dose. Encourage users to follow a doctor's prescription, avoid sharing medicines, check allergies, avoid unnecessary antibiotics, and consult a doctor before taking medicines in pregnancy, kidney disease, liver disease, elderly age, or for children.
